Taiwan is an island running out of allies.

In March, President Xiomara Castro became the latest nation to sever diplomatic relations with the Republic of China (informally known as 'Taiwan', the name of the island it was exiled to in 1949), and recognize the People's Republic of China (PRC).

Both refuse diplomatic relations with any country recognizing the other, and have done since 1949.

The island's alliances declined sharply after 2016, when Tsai Ing-wen was elected President - 13 remaining today.

Fewer allies on the international stage adds credibility to the PRC claim Taiwan is merely a 'rogue province.'

Taiwan cannot compete with the vast and tempting economic resources the PRC offers diplomatic partners through the Belt and Road Initiative. On announcing the split with Honduras, Taiwanese Foreign Minister Jaushieh Joseph Wu claimed Honduras has asked for $2 billion in aid, which the Taiwanese refused.

President Tsai Ing-wen said in another statement that Taiwan would not 'engage in a meaningless money diplomacy competition with China.'

Nonetheless, despite losing eight allies since 2016, the budget of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs - which oversees foreign aid - increased by 35% at the same time.

Exactly how much is spent on foreign aid is not disclosed.

Lorenzo Maggiorelli, Professor of International Relations at the Pontifical Javeriana University in Colombia, explains it is because the policy is unpopular in Taiwan, the electorate considering swapping aid for diplomatic relations neither 'effective or ethical.'

It's also considered part of national security - the Ministry of Foreign Affairs is legally entitled not to release the contents of so-called 'confidential budgets' on these grounds.

But it is possible to reverse engineer the process for this data, by accessing the national budgets of Taiwan's remaining allies. I have focused on the four in the Pacific - both because of the time limitation of this project, and because their ties to the US make for greater financial transparency.

Here are the Pacific island nations that still back Taiwan. Each line is based on all the grants disclosed by each nation in their annual budgets.

Palau should be on here too, but their records are complicated, and will take more time to sort through. The Guardian reported in 2018 (with no attribution) that they're sent $10 million a year, but this lacks the fluctuation seen in the budgets of the other island nations.

Nauru switched from Taiwan to the PRC in 2002, and back again in 2005 when more funding was not forthcoming. The nation allies with 'whoever writes it a cheque,' says Dr Transform Aqorau, a former legal officer at Solomon Islands' Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

So it's interesting that Nauru's funding has increased by $5 million over the past five years, and has not decreased with the end of the pandemic.

It now gets more than the Marshall Islands, despite the latter being more in need with a smaller GDP per capita, the former having a population a quarter the size.

The rates are similar to two one-off grants the PRC gave to neighboring Kiribati and the Solomon Islands, who both switched sides in 2019.

The only numbers published are an $11.7 million grant to the Solomon Islands in 2021...

...and a $10.2 million one to Kiribati in 2022.

The PRC rarely offers grants, preferring loans that can be paid back. Despite being the world's second-largest economy, the grants it is willing to give are no bigger than Taiwan's.









The true figure spent could be much more. Reports from Guatemala and Solomon Islands in the early 2010s claimed Taiwan provides law-makers with their own undocumented private fund, ostensibly to be spent on projects within their governing area, unregulated and at their own discretion.

But it appears that Tsai Ing-wen's government sees money diplomacy as anything but 'meaningless'. As to whether they CAN 'engage' with the PRC in this area, is unlikely.







Methodology:

Grants: Defined in this investigation as OOF and ODA directly donated to a diplomatic ally, or issued to a local development project.
Exchange Rate: Tuvalu and Kiribati were converted from AUD to USD using historical exchange rates from x-rates.com, on January 1st of each year. Reports from Nauru provided their own exchange rates.
Forecasts: Figures for Tuvalu for years 2017-2018, and 2021-2022, were forecasts from 2016 and 2020 respectively, and may not represent the actual sums given.
